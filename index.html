Ms. Friedsam's College Essay Workshop

Your story matters.
Let's learn how to tell it.

The college essay is your chance to show admissions officers who you are beyond grades and test scores — a living, breathing portrait of you as a student, a leader, and a human being.

What is the college essay?

No matter the topic or prompt, all college essays essentially answer two questions: Who are you? And What do you care about?

Reflect your personality

The essay should sound like you — in your own voice, telling a story only you can tell.

Reveal your character

Shed light on who you are as a student, a leader, and a human being through lively, specific anecdotes.

Go beyond the application

This is your chance to share what no grade, test score, or activity list can communicate.

What admissions officers say

"In the essay, you have to be on stage. You can share the stage with others, but the audience wants to see you."

— Vassar College

"If one views the grades and program of study as a skeleton, then the essays put flesh and blood on the skeleton. The totality is what makes an applicant become a real person."

— University of Virginia

"Of all the essays I read, roughly 70% do nothing for the student; 20% help the student by bringing the application to life; and 5% actually hurt the applicant's chances."

— Emory University

🎯 The goal: be part of the 20% that brings your application to life.

Do's and Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Reveal something about your character and personality
  • Answer the actual question or prompt
  • Open with a catchy, memorable first sentence
  • Write about something small in scale — a story only you can tell
  • Be honest. Let the reader hear your real voice
  • Use language you're actually comfortable with
  • Write about something that impacted your private life

✗ Don't

  • Write a list of your attributes or activities
  • Use the same essay for every application (check the college name!)
  • Over-rely on a thesaurus to sound sophisticated
  • Write about someone else's tragedy — you should be the star
  • Use gimmicks like haiku or third-person narration
  • Mention your SAT scores or grades in the essay
  • Let someone else do too much editorial work on it

Your process

  1. Reflect on your personal qualities and identity

  2. Brainstorm specific incidents, passions, and moments of growth

  3. Free write about one incident and let the story unfold

  4. Share with someone who can offer honest, helpful feedback

  5. Revise and rewrite — the magic happens in revision

Reflection questions

Take your time with each of these. The goal is to dig up specific memories and moments — not to write a perfect essay yet.

What do you care most about in the world?

Think broadly — ideas, people, places, causes, creative work, a craft. What would you talk about for hours without getting tired?

What experience has shaped who you are?

It doesn't have to be dramatic. Small moments can reveal just as much as big ones.

What do your closest friends or family know about you that admissions officers don't?

The sides of you that don't appear on a transcript — what are they?

How have you been a leader?

Leadership isn't always a title. Think of times you've guided, influenced, or inspired someone — formally or informally.

Look back at your top 10 qualities. Which of your answers above connect to those traits? That intersection is gold for your essay.

Activity 2 · Brainstorming

Find your story.

Great essays start with specific, concrete moments. Use these timed prompts to uncover the stories that only you can tell. Set a timer and just write — don't edit, don't second-guess.

1

A place you love to go

3 minutes · write without stopping

Think in sensory details:

  • What does it look like?
  • What does it sound like?
  • What does it smell like?
  • What does it make you feel?
  • Why is it one of your favorite places?
2

A memory that makes you feel good

Free write · let the details flow

Reach for a specific memory — not a general feeling. Where were you? Who was there? What happened, step by step?

3

Something you have conquered

Dig into the struggle

Explore:

  • What was the struggle?
  • What did you put in?
  • Was there a pivotal moment?
  • What changed in you?
4

Something you love to do

(You don't even have to be good at it)

Get really granular:

  • Describe what you do, in detail
  • Are you good at it?
  • How does it make you feel?
  • What does this reveal about who you are?

Activity 3 · Drafting

Get it on the page.

Good essays are rewritten, not just written. Here are two critical skills that will elevate your draft from ordinary to memorable.

That's So Cliché!

Clichés are phrases that have been overused so much they've lost all originality and impact. In college essays, they're a sign of lazy writing — and admissions officers notice.

Finish these phrases (and then never use them in your essay):

  • "The straw that broke..."
  • "Don't cry over..."
  • "I like to think outside..."
  • "Don't put all your eggs..."
  • "Kill two birds..."
  • "Like finding a needle..."

A real example of cliché overload:

"When it comes to school activities, I prefer to be a big fish in a little pond. Whether I am promoting events for Online Gaming Club or leading a cake decorating class in Baking Club, I am constantly working to raise the bar and make my mark. At the end of the day, I always give 110% in order to set a good example for younger members about living life to the fullest."

Bolded phrases are clichés. Can you count them all?

How to fix a cliché:

  1. Scan your draft and highlight any overused phrases you find.

  2. Ask yourself: what am I actually trying to say here?

  3. Rewrite it in your own specific words — the more concrete and personal, the better.

Building your arc: Scene & Reflection

The most powerful college essays move between scene (showing what happened) and reflection (explaining what it meant). This back-and-forth is what makes a story feel alive — and makes you feel like a thoughtful, self-aware person.

Open "in scene"

Drop the reader right into a specific moment. Use narrative details: setting, dialogue, character description.

Sprinkle in reflection

Pause to share something you realize now, but maybe didn't at the time. Why does this moment matter?

Return to scene

Go back into descriptive, narrative writing. Keep the story moving forward.

Concluding reflection

Land on what you've processed about this experience — how it shaped you or prepared you for what's next.

Check your draft against these questions:

  • Are you building scene anywhere? If so — is it conveying what you want?
  • If not — where could you add setting details, dialogue, or character description?
  • Are you including reflection? Where?
  • If not — where could you add a moment of insight or meaning?

Sample essay analysis

Reading strong essays helps you understand what makes them work. For each sample essay your teacher shares, practice this analysis to sharpen your own writing instincts.

For each sample essay, ask:

What qualities emerge?

Write 2–4 character traits that come through about the applicant.

Where is the "scene"?

Identify a moment where you feel like you're right there with the writer.

Where is the reflection?

Find a sentence where the writer steps back and tells you what something meant.

What's memorable?

What's the one thing about this essay you'll still remember tomorrow?
